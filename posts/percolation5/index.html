Last time we outlined the four steps of the proof of Harris-Kesten and worked through the first. This time, we’ll do the second: showing that for p>1/2p>1/2, there is an infinite open cluster with probability 1, allowing us to conclude that pH=12p_H=\frac{1}{2}. We begin with two lemmas.

1. Some Lemmas

Recalling our discussion of the combinatorial hypercube, we say that a bond ee is pivotal for an event EE in a configuration ω\omega if switching the state of ee takes ω\omega into or out of EE. The influence of ee on EE is Ip(e,E)=ℙp(e is pivotal for E).I_p(e,E)=\mathbb{P}_p(e\text{ is pivotal for }E). For EE increasing, this simplifies to ℙp(ω+∈E,ω−∉E),\mathbb{P}_p(\omega^+\in E, \omega^{-}\not\in E), where ω±\omega^{\pm} are configurations that differ from ω\omega only at ee.

Lemma 1. Let RR be an m×nm\times n rectangle in ℤ2\mathbb{Z}^2. For a bond ee in RR, Ip(e,H(R))≤2ℙ1/2(r(C0)≥min{m/2−1,(n−1)/2})I_p(e, H(R))\leq 2\mathbb{P}_{1/2}(r(C_0)\geq \min\{m/2-1, (n-1)/2\}) for 0<p<10<p<1.

Proof. For this proof, we ignore all bonds outside RR. The argument is a little weird: we’ll prove that Ip(e,H(R))≤2ℙp(r(C0)≥m/2−1)I_p(e, H(R))\leq 2\mathbb{P}_p(r(C_0)\geq m/2-1) and Op(e,H(R))≤2ℙ1−p(r(C0)≥(n−1)/2).O_p(e, H(R))\leq 2\mathbb{P}_{1-p}(r(C_0)\geq (n-1)/2). Then, noting that ℙp(r(C0)≥a)\mathbb{P}_p(r(C_0)\geq a) is an increasing function of pp, the first allows us to conclude for p≤1/2p\leq 1/2 and the second for p≥1/2p\geq 1/2.

Since ω+∈H(R)\omega^+\in H(R) and ω−∉H(R)\omega^-\not\in H(R), any horizontal crossing of RR uses ee. Thus, in ω\omega, each endpoint of ee is joined by an open path to either end of RR, at least one of which must have length ≥m/2−1\geq m/2-1, so the first bound given above follows.

Since ω−∉H(R)\omega^-\not \in H(R), a previous lemma tells us that ω−∈V(Rh)\omega^-\in V(R^h). Similarly, ω+∉V(Rh)\omega^+\not \in V(R^h), so ω−\omega^- contains an open dual path crossing RhR^h and using the dual edge of ee. So that edge has an endpoint which is the start of an open dual path of length (n−1)/2(n-1)/2, and so we get the second bound stated above. ◼\blacksquare

2. Kesten’s Theorem

This is the last piece in the proof that pH(ℤ2)=12p_H(\mathbb{Z}^2)=\frac{1}{2}.

Theorem 3 (Kesten). Let E∞E_{\infty} be the event that there is an infinite open cluster. Then for bond percolation in ℤ2\mathbb{Z}^2, if p>1/2p>1/2, we have ℙp(E∞)=1.\mathbb{P}_p(E_{\infty})=1.

Proof. Take p>1/2p>1/2 and choose γ\gamma (depending on pp) and n0n_0 be as in the second lemma with ρ=2\rho=2. Choose some large nn, and let R1,⋯R_1,\cdots be a family of rectangles whose bottom left corner is the origin and which have sides of length 2kn2^kn and 2k+1n2^{k+1}n. The longer side is the vertical for even kk and the horizontal for odd kk.

Suppose EkE_k is the event that RkR_k is crossed in the longer direction by an open path. Any two such paths must meet, so if every EkE_k holds, so does E∞E_{\infty} (since the open cluster containing the origin has unbounded radius). Now we write ℙp(E∞)≥ℙp(⋂k=0∞Ek)=1−ℙp(⋃k=0∞Ekc)≥1−∑k=0∞ℙp(Ekc).\mathbb{P}_p(E_{\infty})\geq \mathbb{P}_p\left(\bigcap_{k=0}^{\infty} E_k\right)=1-\mathbb{P}_p\left(\bigcup_{k=0}^{\infty} E_k^c\right)\geq 1-\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \mathbb{P}_p(E_k^c). But by the final lemma of the previous section, and the fact that nn is arbitrarily large, ∑k=0∞ℙ(Ekc)≤∑k=0∞(2kn)−γ=n−γ1−2−γ<1.\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \mathbb{P}(E_k^c)\leq \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (2^kn)^{-\gamma}=\frac{n^{-\gamma}}{1-2^{-\gamma}}<1. So ℙp(E∞)>0\mathbb{P}_p(E_{\infty})>0, and thus 1. ◼\blacksquare

The result proven at the end of the last post was that pH>12p_H>\frac{1}{2} while this result shows that pH≤12p_H\leq \frac{1}{2}, so we conclude that in fact pH=12p_H=\frac{1}{2}. In the next and final post, we’ll turn our attention to pTp_T.
