We’re ready to start proving the Harris-Kesten theorem, so from now on, everything we say is about bond percolation in ℤ2\mathbb{Z} ^2. The global structure of the argument is in three steps:

  1. Show that θ(1/2)=0\theta(1/2)=0 (this implies pH≥1/2p_H\geq 1/2)
  2. Show that for p>1/2p>1/2, there is an infinite open cluster with probability 1 (this implies pH≤1/2p_H\leq 1/2)
  3. Show that χ(p)<∞\chi(p)<\infty for p<1/2p<1/2 (this implies that pT≥1/2p_T\geq 1/2)
  4. Conclude that pT=pH=1/2p_T=p_H=1/2

This post will accomplish the first step.

1. Crossing Probabilities

We start with some more definitions: a rectangle RR in ℤ2\mathbb{Z} ^2 is a set of sites of the form [a,b]×[c,d][a,b]\times [c,d]. We say this rectangle has size k×ℓk\times \ell, where k=b−a+1,ℓ=d−c+1k=b-a+1, \ell=d-c+1. It contains kℓk\ell sites and 2kℓ−k−ℓ2k\ell-k-\ell bonds.

This rectangle has two duals: a horizontal dual Rh=[a+0.5,b−0.5]×[c−0.5,d+0.5]R^h=[a+0.5, b-0.5]\times [c-0.5, d+0.5] and a vertical dual Rv=[a−0.5,b+0.5]×[c+0.5,d−0.5].R^v=[a-0.5, b+0.5]\times [c+0.5, d-0.5]. We have that RhR^h is a k−1×ℓ+1k-1\times \ell+1 rectangle and RvR^v is a k+1×ℓ−1k+1\times \ell-1 rectangle. Additionally, (Rh)v=(Rv)h=R.\left(R^h\right)^v=\left(R^v\right)^h=R.

Finally, we define an open horizontal crossing of a rectangle RR to be an open path in RR connecting a site (a,x)(a,x) to a site (b,y)(b,y), and similarly for an open vertical crossing. We write H(R)H(R) and V(R)V(R) for the events that such crossings exist.

The following lemma is, according to Bollobas, the “reason” why pH=1/2p_H=1/2. It is intuitively clear, but working through all the details is a bit of slog, so let’s just take it at face value.

Lemma 1. Let RR be a rectangle in ℤ2\mathbb{Z} ^2 or its dual. Then exactly one of H(R)H(R) and V(Rh)V(R^h) holds.

Why exactly is this lemma the “reason” cited above? The following corollary sheds some light on that.

Corollary 2. If RR and R′R' are k×ℓ−1k\times \ell-1 and k−1×ℓk-1\times \ell rectangles in ℤ2\mathbb{Z} ^2, then ℙp(H(R))+ℙ1−p(V(R′))=1.\mathbb{P} _p(H(R))+\mathbb{P} _{1-p}(V(R'))=1.

Proof. The bonds of the dual are open when the bonds of the original graph are closed and vice versa. So by Lemma 1, each configuration of bonds will satisfy exactly one of H(R)H(R) and V(Rh)V(R^h), so ℙ(H(R))+ℙ(V(Rh))=1.\mathbb{P} (H(R))+\mathbb{P} (V(R^h))=1. But RhR^h is indistinguishable from R′R', so the result follows. ◼\blacksquare

Now if we take k=ℓk=\ell in this corollary, we get that for an n+1×nn+1\times n rectangle RR, ℙ1/2(H(R))+ℙ1/2(V(R′))=1.\mathbb{P} _{1/2}(H(R))+\mathbb{P} _{1/2}(V(R'))=1. But because the rectangle R′R' has dimension n×n+1n\times n+1, the event V(R′)V(R') has the same probability as H(R′)H(R'), so they are both equal to 1/21/2.

So the intuition is somehow that when p>1/2p>1/2, it’s more likely you have a horizontal crossing than not, so you get an infinite open cluster. When p<1/2p<1/2, it’s less likely than not, so you don’t. The critical behavior is at exactly p=1/2p=1/2.

More importantly, we get that if SS is an n×nn\times n square, ℙ1/2(V(S))=ℙ1/2(H(S))≥12,\mathbb{P} _{1/2}(V(S))=\mathbb{P} _{1/2}(H(S))\geq \frac{1}{2}, where we have the inequality because this event must be more likely than the crossing for an n×n+1n\times n+1 rectangle. This is a bound on the crossing probabilities of a square. We now have to extend this result to rectangles.

2. Squares to Rectangles

This next lemma relates crossings of squares to crossings of rectangles. We use the notation [n]=[0,n][n]=[0,n].

Lemma 3. Consider R=[m]×[2n]R=[m]\times [2n], an m×2nm\times 2n rectangle with m≥nm\geq n. Let X(R)X(R) be the event that there are open paths P1,P2P_1, P_2 such that P1P_1 crosses the n×nn\times n square S=[n]×[n]S=[n]\times [n] from top to bottom and P2P_2 lies within RR and joins some site on P1P_1 to the right hand side of RR. Then ℙp(X(R))≥ℙp(H(R))ℙp(V(S))2.\mathbb{P} _p(X(R))\geq \frac{\mathbb{P} _p(H(R))\mathbb{P} _p(V(S))}{2}.

Proof. Suppose V(S)V(S) occurs, so an open path P0P_0 crosses SS from top to bottom. Such a path cuts SS into two pieces, one on the left and on the right. Let LV(S)LV(S) be the left-most open vertical crossing (when one exists). For any P1P_1, the event {LV(S)=P1}\{LV(S)=P_1\} does not depend on bonds to the right of P1P_1.

The key claim is that ℙp(X(R)∣LV(S)=P1)≥ℙ(H(R))2.\mathbb{P} _p(X(R)\mid LV(S)=P_1)\geq \frac{\mathbb{P} (H(R))}{2}. Since V(S)V(S) is a disjoint union of events of the form {LV(S)=P1}\{LV(S)=P_1\}, this implies that ℙp(X(R)∣V(S))≥ℙp(H(R))2,\mathbb{P} _p(X(R)\mid V(S))\geq\frac{\mathbb{P} _p(H(R))}{2}, which rearranges to ℙp(X(R)∩V(S))≥ℙp(H(R))ℙp(V(S))2.\mathbb{P} _p(X(R)\cap V(S))\geq \frac{\mathbb{P} _p(H(R))\mathbb{P} _p(V(S))}{2}. But X(R)X(R) occurring means that V(S)V(S) does as well, so ℙp(X(R)∩V(S))=ℙp(X(R))\mathbb{P} _p(X(R)\cap V(S))=\mathbb{P} _p(X(R)), and the lemma is proven.

For the claim, let PP be the path (that may or may not be open) formed by the union of P1P_1 and it’s reflection P1′P_1' in the horizontal axis of RR, with a bond joining P1P_1 to P1′P_1'. This path is a vertical crossing of the rectangle [n]×[2n][n]\times [2n].

Now, there is an open path P3P_3 crossing RR horizontally with probability ℙp(H(R))\mathbb{P} _p(H(R)). This path must meet PP, and the probability it does so at a site of P1P_1 is at least ℙp(H(R))/2\mathbb{P} _p(H(R))/2. Thus the event that there is an open path P2P_2 in RR to the right of PP joining a site of P1P_1 to the right end of RR has probability at least ℙp(H(R))/2\mathbb{P} _p(H(R))/2. This event depends only bonds to the right of PP, and all such bonds in SS are to the right of P1P_1. These bonds are independent of LV(S)=P1LV(S)=P_1, so ℙp(Y(P1)∣LV(S)=P1)=ℙp(Y(P1))≥ℙp(H(R))/2.\mathbb{P} _p(Y(P_1)\mid LV(S)=P_1)=\mathbb{P} _p(Y(P_1))\geq \mathbb{P} _p(H(R))/2.

If Y(P1)Y(P_1) and LV(S)=P1LV(S)=P_1 both occur, then X(R)X(R) does as well. So ℙp(X(R)∣LV(S)=P1)≥ℙp(H(R))/2,\mathbb{P} _p(X(R)\mid LV(S)=P_1)\geq \mathbb{P} _p(H(R))/2, proving the claim and thus the lemma. ◼\blacksquare

Remark 1. If we instead take an m1×2nm_1\times 2n rectangle RR and an m2×2nm_2\times 2n rectangle R′R', this proof will show that h(m1+m2−n,2n)≥h(m1,2n)h(m2,2n)25,h(m_1+m_2-n, 2n)\geq \frac{h(m_1, 2n)h(m_2,2n)}{2^5}, and we will use this formulation later on.

3. Thinning the Rectangles Out

With this lemma, we can now bound the crossing probabilities for non-square rectangles. In particular, we look at a 3n×2n3n\times 2n rectangle. For convenience, let hp(m,n)=ℙp(H(R))h_p(m,n)=\mathbb{P} _p(H(R)), and since it’s the case we really care about, h(m,n)=h1/2(m,n)h(m,n)=h_{1/2}(m,n).

Corollary 4. We have h(3n,2n)≥2−7h(3n, 2n)\geq 2^{-7}.

Proof. Take two square 2n×2n2n\times 2n rectangles R,R′R, R' that overlap in an n×2nn\times 2n strip. This n×2nn\times 2n strip contains two n×nn\times n squares; call SS the smaller of these. (Again, draw the picture!) Let X′(R′)X'(R') be the event X(R)X(R) described above, but reflected horizontally. Then we apply Lemma 4 to RR (which is coincidentally a square) to get ℙp(X′(R′))=ℙp(X(R))≥ℙp(H(R))ℙp(V(S))2.\mathbb{P} _p(X'(R'))=\mathbb{P} _p(X(R))\geq \frac{\mathbb{P} _p(H(R))\mathbb{P} _p(V(S))}{2}.

Now, we call back to Harris’s lemma from the previous post: the events X(R),X′(R′),H(S)X(R), X'(R'), H(S) are all increasingly and positive correlated. If they all hold, so does H(R∪R′)H(R\cup R'), and so we have h(3n,2n)=ℙ1/2(H(R∪R′))≥ℙ1/2(X′(R′))ℙ1/2(X(R))ℙ1/2(H(S)).h(3n,2n)=\mathbb{P} _{1/2}(H(R\cup R'))\geq \mathbb{P} _{1/2}(X'(R'))\mathbb{P} _{1/2}(X(R))\mathbb{P} _{1/2}(H(S)). This is then ≥ℙ1/2(H(R))2ℙ1/2(V(S))2ℙ1/2(H(S))4.\geq \frac{\mathbb{P} _{1/2}(H(R))^2\mathbb{P} _{1/2}(V(S))^2\mathbb{P} _{1/2}(H(S))}{4}.

But by our observation at the end of Section 1, this is at least (0.52)34=2−7,\frac{(0.5^2)^3}{4}=2^{-7}, as desired. blacksquareblacksquare

A 3n×2n3n\times 2n rectangle isn’t quite thing enough for our purposes, but the rest of the thinning can be done using the remark after Lemma 3. We have h(5n,2n)≥h(3m,2n)225≥2−19,h(5n,2n)\geq \frac{h(3m,2n)^2}{2^5}\geq 2^{-19}, and so h(6n,2n)≥h(5n,2n)h(2n,2n)25≥2−25,h(6n,2n)\geq \frac{h(5n,2n)h(2n,2n)}{2^5}\geq 2^{-25}, and this is the bound that our proof of θ(1/2)=0\theta(1/2)=0 will hinge on.

4. Harris’s Theorem

Theorem 5 (Harris). For bond percolation on ℤ2\mathbb{Z} ^2, θ(1/2)=0\theta(1/2)=0.

Proof. Let r(C0)=sup{d(x,0)∣x∈C0}.r(C_0)=\sup\{d(x,0)\mid x\in C_0\}. The actual result we’ll show is that ℙ1/2(r(C0)≥n)≤n−c\mathbb{P} _{1/2}(r(C_0)\geq n)\leq n^{-c} for a constant cc, and the stated result follows from this.

When p=1/2p=1/2, bonds in the dual lattice are open with probability 1/21/2, so by the computation above, the probability a 6n×2n6n\times 2n rectangle has a crossing the long way is at least 2−252^{-25}. Consider 4 such rectangles oriented to form an annulus around the origin, with the “hole” in the annulus being a 2n×2n2n\times 2n square centered at the origin.

By Harris’s lemma, the probability each of these rectangles is crossed the long way by an open path in the dual is at least ϵ=2−100\epsilon=2^{-100}. The union of these paths is an open dual cycle around the annulus.

Let AkA_k be the square annulus centered on (0.5,0.5)(0.5, 0.5) with inner radius 4k4^k and outer radius 3⋅4k3\cdot 4^k. Let EkE_k be the event that AkA_k contains an open dual cycle surrounding the inside of AkA_k, and thus the origin.

By our argument above, ℙ(Ek)≥ϵ\mathbb{P} (E_k)\geq \epsilon for each kk. All of the EkE_k are independent, and if EkE_k holds, then there is no path from inside AkA_k to outside AkA_k, so r(C0)≤1+3⋅4k≤4k+1.r(C_0)\leq 1+3\cdot 4^k\leq 4^{k+1}.

Then, ℙ(r(C0)≥4ℓ+1)≤ℙ1/2(⋂k=1ℓEkc)=∏k=1ℓℙ1/2(Ekc)≤(1−ϵ)ℓ,\mathbb{P} \left(r(C_0)\geq 4^{\ell+1}\right)\leq \mathbb{P} _{1/2}\left(\bigcap_{k=1}^{\ell} E_k^c\right)=\prod_{k=1}^{\ell} \mathbb{P} _{1/2}(E_k^c)\leq (1-\epsilon)^{\ell}, which is the desired bound. ◼\blacksquare
